Ring-a-ring-a-rosies


The Nursery Rhyme we know today

Ring around the rosie, has swept the world to be one of the most well known nursery rhymes of the age. Scripted in the year 1881 in England, kids around the world gather in a circle by holding hands, and sing while moving in a circle:

Ring-a-ring o’ roses Pocket full of posies A-tissue, A-tissue We all fall down Alongside this more common version of the nursery rhyme, there are multiple variations, all conveying slightly different meanings. One variation of the nursery rhyme shown above includes: Ring-a-ring o’ roses Pocket full of posies Ashes! Ashes! We all fall down

This nursery rhyme has been perceived by most people to be a fun and playful tune. The children partaking in this rhyme enjoy it, ending it in a fall. At first listen, this rhyme for children seems to have a quite simple and straightforward meaning. ‘Ring-a-ring o’ roses’, means a ring of roses. ‘Pocket full of poses’, represents a bunch of poses which are just flowers. ‘A-tissue, A-tissue, We all fall down’ interprets to the reader that they are about to sneeze, and have fallen over. While this rhyme does have a playful ring and everybody enjoys it, there lies a hidden, dark meaning, masked by its jovial exterior.

Ring-a-ring-a-rosie nursery Rhyme

The Nursery Rhyme's dark Meaning revealed

All the lyrics to this nursery rhyme have a meaning which is nothing like what is interpreted by a viewer. The origins of Ring around the rosie was as said before in the 17th century, and you will find that the meaning isn't as pleasant as it seems. It refers to the Bubonic plague, commonly referred to as the Black Death. The Black Death was a pandemic which ravaged through Europe and Asia in the mid 12th century. From the span of 1347-1351, this deadly disease managed to wipe out 25 million people in Europe, around ⅓ of the population of the continent. Ring around the rosie has 4 lines in the whole rhyme, each one with a disturbing meaning involving the bubonic plague. ‘Ring-a-ring o’ roses’ conveys the red rashes and scars which are a symptom of the Black Death. ‘Pocket full of posies’ refers to the children who hold a posy of herbs to ward off the plague or its smell. ‘A-tissue, A-tissue’ mentions the side effect of sneezing. And the last one. The worst one of them all. ‘We all fall down’, refers to the death of someone contracting the Bubonic Plague.
